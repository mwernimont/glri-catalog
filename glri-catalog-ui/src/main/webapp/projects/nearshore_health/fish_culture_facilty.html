Great Lakes Restoration Initiative (GLRI)


Native Fish Restoration in the Lake Ontario Basin

Introduction

USGS is taking an innovative approach to restoration, simultaneously restoring two native fish species at different food web levels in Lake Ontario. Historically, the Lake Ontario population of Atlantic salmon represented the largest freshwater population of salmon in the world. Overfishing and loss of spawning habitat has since led to their extirpation. A state-of-the-art fish culture facility has been established at the USGS Tunison Laboratory of Aquatic Science (Cortland, NY), where USGS scientists are working to develop effective rearing and stocking techniques for Atlantic salmon and bloater (a type of herring). Scientists have identified juvenile salmon release locations with the highest probability for success of capturing returning adult salmon; all part of establishing a Lake Ontario egg source to perpetuate the project. The first release of fall fingerling salmon occurred in fall 2011 with plans to release the first “Lake Ontario strain” salmon in 2016. Bloater release is scheduled to commence in 2012. This is a critical first step in restoring a resilient native fish community in Lake Ontario, strengthening both the local ecosystem and local economy.

Funding for the USGS Tunison Laboratory’s enhanced salmon rearing capabilities (including a new UV treatment facility) comes from the Great Lakes Restoration Initiative and a 2005 Congressional appropriation. The water treatment facility will use ultraviolet disinfection to prevent any possible spread of disease, permitting wild fish and eggs to be held and disease-free fish to be reared for restoration release. An open house to showcase the new facility is scheduled for January 2012.
